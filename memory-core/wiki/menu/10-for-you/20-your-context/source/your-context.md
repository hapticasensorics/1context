# Your Context

Your Context is the page a human teammate or AI agent should read before doing
serious work with you. It should become more specific over time, but it starts
with the shape of the record: what to notice, where to place it, and when a
pattern is stable enough to preserve.

## Working Style

Record how you move through work: how you scope, correct, decide, interrupt,
resume, parallelize, or ask collaborators to think with you. Promote only
patterns with repeated evidence or one very explicit instruction.

## Coding Style

Record code-level preferences: naming, comments, formatting, testing habits,
dependency taste, refactor thresholds, and what "done" feels like in code.

## Engineering Philosophy

Record broader technical beliefs: what should be simple, what should be bought
instead of built, what belongs in infrastructure, what belongs in product logic,
and which tradeoffs you consistently choose.

## Preferences

Record durable preferences about tools, workflows, communication, design,
planning, pacing, and collaboration.

## Taste

Record aesthetic judgments and product instincts: what feels professional, what
feels cheap, what should be quiet, what should be expressive, and which examples
you return to.

## Desires

Record what you are moving toward: products you want to exist, capabilities you
want the system to gain, and experiences that would make the work feel alive.

## Recurring Ideas

Record ideas that keep reappearing across projects. These often become topic
pages once they have enough evidence.

## Habits

Record repeated behaviors that affect collaboration: how you review, how you
switch contexts, how you use agents, and what tends to help or hurt momentum.

## Coworkers

Record recurring collaborators, their roles, working relationships, and context
that helps agents avoid losing the human map around the work.

## Infra & Tooling

Record the practical environment: repos, machines, services, local paths,
permissions, build systems, release channels, and operational rituals.

## Standing Requests

Record instructions you give repeatedly. Use direct imperative language when the
request is meant to guide future collaborators.

## Notes For AI Agents

Record AI-specific collaboration rules: when to search, when to inspect files,
when to ask, how to use hooks, how to cite evidence, and how to avoid replaying
old mistakes.

## Life Story

This section is reserved for a longer, less frequent narrative rewrite. Do not
fill it from a single observation. It should synthesize larger arcs once the
system has enough history.
